# Recruitment Reference

## Types of Recruitment

### Direct Recruitment

**Recruit from your own network / social media**
- Post screeners in relevant communities (LinkedIn, Facebook groups, WhatsApp, Slack)
- Pros: Low cost, fast turnaround, easy to reach relevant audiences
- Cons: Risk of homogenous sample, relies on favors, limited screening control

**Recruit existing product users**
- Use CRM database, in-app prompts, live intercepts on the product
- Pros: Highly relevant participants, genuine users
- Cons: Effort to maintain database, may introduce familiarity bias

**Internal teammates / colleagues**
- Use when time and budget are very limited
- Pros: Instant, free
- Cons: Significant sampling bias, familiarity affects behavior, should not be default

**Guerrilla recruiting (in the wild)**
- Approach people in physical locations relevant to the product context
- Pros: Fast, low cost, naturalistic
- Cons: Little screening control, weather/location dependent, not for detailed studies

**Live intercepts (users currently on the site)**
- Pop-up on product inviting live users to participate
- Pros: Real users, in-context
- Cons: Low acceptance rate, requires researcher availability, disrupts user experience

### Indirect Recruitment

**Third-party recruitment firm / research panels**
- Hand screener to a recruiting firm; they source and schedule participants
- Pros: High-quality screening, handles logistics, provides replacements for no-shows
- Cons: Expensive (typically $50–$200+ per participant)

**Online user testing platforms (Respondent.io, UserTesting, etc.)**
- Upload screener to platform; participants self-select and are matched
- Pros: Fast, large pool, good for remote unmoderated studies
- Cons: Costly, panel fatigue (same participants across many studies)

**Client-sourced participants**
- Client provides access to their user database
- Pros: Highly relevant users
- Cons: Less control over screening quality, scheduling depends on client

## Choosing a Recruitment Method

| Situation | Recommended Method |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Low budget, small team | Direct: social media + network outreach |
| Need real product users | Live intercepts + CRM outreach |
| Tight timeline | Guerrilla recruiting or user testing platform |
| High-stakes study, larger budget | Third-party panel or Respondent.io |
| Niche or hard-to-reach population | Client-sourced or specialized panel |

## Screener Best Practices

### 1. Keep it short (max 5 questions)
Participants abandon long screeners. The most effective screeners are the shortest. No ideal number — calibrate to how narrow your criteria are.

### 2. Ask qualifying questions first
Filter out clearly ineligible respondents early — don't send them all 5 questions if question 1 disqualifies them.

### 3. Avoid yes/no questions
Non-ideal participants have a 50% chance of guessing correctly. Use multiple choice with distractors.

| ❌ Don't | ✅ Do |
|---------|------|
| Do you work in marketing? a) Yes b) No | Which field do you work in? a) Marketing b) Finance c) Technology d) Healthcare e) None of the above |

### 4. Be specific with frequencies
Don't use "often" or "rarely" without defining them.

| ❌ Don't | ✅ Do |
|---------|------|
| How often do you use this app? a) Often b) Rarely c) Sometimes | How often? a) Daily (5+ times/day) b) Daily (1–4 times) c) A few times a week d) A few times a month e) Rarely f) Never |

### 5. No overlapping answer ranges
| ❌ Don't | ✅ Do |
|---------|------|
| Age: 20–40, 40–60, 60+ | Age: 20–39, 40–59, 60+ |

### 6. Cover the full continuum
Don't leave gaps in your frequency or range options. Every possible respondent should find an option that fits them.

### 7. Don't reveal the study focus
Screener questions should hide what you're testing by mixing the real criteria among plausible distractors.
❌ "Have you used AllEvents.in?" (reveals the product immediately)
✅ "Which of the following event discovery platforms have you used in the past 6 months? a) Meetup b) AllEvents.in c) Eventbrite d) Facebook Events e) None of these"

### 8. Limit open-ended questions
Too many open-ended questions cause participant fatigue and create manual screening burden. Use them only when multiple choice can't capture the answer.

### 9. Exclude designers and researchers
Unless the product is designed for them. Designers answer based on professional opinion rather than natural behavior.

### 10. Logistics questions at the end
Confirm device, internet connection, availability for study dates — these are easy and signal commitment.

## Over-Recruitment Strategy
Always recruit 20–25% more participants than needed to account for:
- No-shows (common, especially for remote studies)
- Disqualification on arrival (participant doesn't match screener in person)
- Technical failures

Example: Need 8 participants → recruit 10.

## Non-Qualifying Respondent Message
Always send a polite, future-forward message to people who don't qualify:
> "Thank you for your interest. We don't have a study that matches your profile right now, but we'd love to keep your details and reach out when a suitable opportunity comes up."

This builds a participant database and preserves goodwill.

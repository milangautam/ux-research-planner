# Discussion Guide Reference

## What a Discussion Guide Is

A discussion guide (also called a protocol) is the moderator's script. It ensures every session covers the same ground, in a logical order, without introducing bias. It is not a rigid script to read word-for-word — it's a structured scaffold the moderator navigates flexibly.

## The 9-Step Structure

### Step 1 — Introduction
- Put participant at ease immediately
- Manage expectations (what the study is about at a high level, without revealing hypotheses)
- Warm, conversational tone

### Step 2 — Study Goal
- Explain *why* you're doing this study (without revealing what you're testing for)
- Frame it as: "We want to make sure this works well for people like you"
- This reduces social desirability bias — participant understands it's the product being tested, not them

### Step 3 — Think-Aloud Protocol
- Explain that you want them to narrate their thoughts as they go
- Give a concrete example of what think-aloud sounds like
- Practice with a neutral example (not the actual product)

### Step 4 — Comfort Check
- Physical comfort (water, seating)
- Open questions from participant before starting

### Step 5 — Recording Permission
- Ask explicitly
- Explain who will see it and how it will be used
- Reassure confidentiality

### Step 6 — Warm-Up Questions
- 3–5 questions about their background, habits, and existing behaviors
- Related to the product domain but not the specific product
- Goal: understand context, build rapport, establish baseline mental model
- Examples: "What do you normally do when you need to [task]?" / "What tools or apps do you use for [task]?"

### Step 7 — Task Scenarios
For each task:
- **Context:** Set up a realistic scenario. Make it feel natural, not like a test instruction.
- **Task:** State what they need to accomplish — frame it as a goal, not a click instruction
- **Moderator observation cues:** What behavior to watch for (not to say aloud)
- **Follow-up questions:** Open-ended probes after the task
- **Post-task questions:** Did this meet expectations? What made it easy or hard?

### Step 8 — Post-Study Debrief
- Overall impressions of the experience
- What worked vs. what didn't
- What they wished they'd seen
- Any final thoughts

### Step 9 — Close
- Thank participant genuinely
- Explain next steps (data is used to improve, will remain anonymous)
- Handle incentive

## Task Scenario Writing Tips

Good task scenarios:
- Set context with a realistic situation ("Imagine you've just moved to a new city and want to…")
- State the goal, not the steps ("Find an event this weekend you'd want to attend")
- Don't name UI elements ("click the search bar") — let the participant discover them
- Don't hint at the answer ("Use the filter to narrow down…")
- Feel like real life, not a test

## Sentiment Dimensions to Cover

Your questions across the full guide must probe all five of these:

| Dimension | What to look for | Example probe |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Intuitiveness** | Did it feel natural without being told? | "What was your first instinct when you saw this screen?" |
| **Satisfaction** | Did it meet expectations? | "What are your overall thoughts about this experience?" |
| **Confidence** | Did they feel sure about what to do? | "How did you feel while completing this step?" |
| **Value** | Did they see why this product matters to them? | "How do you think this fits into how you normally do [task]?" |
| **Effortlessness** | Did it feel easy? | "What, if anything, made this feel difficult or easy?" |

## Mental Model Dimensions to Explore

| Dimension | What it reveals | How to probe |
|-----------|----------------|--------------|
| **Mental Model** | What they expected before seeing the UI | "Before you clicked, what did you think would happen?" |
| **Discoverability** | Whether they found features naturally | Observe — did they find it without help? |
| **Understandability** | Whether they understood what things meant | "Can you describe what you understand this section to be for?" |
| **Learnability** | Whether they figured things out as they went | Observe — did they get better over time? |
| **Usability** | Whether they could complete tasks | Observe task success/failure + ask post-task |

## Bias Rules

### 1. Don't suggest an answer
❌ "How well did this save you time?"
✅ "How might this affect your efficiency, if at all?"

### 2. Don't name UI elements
❌ "What do you think of this button?"
✅ "Did you notice this element here [point]? What are your thoughts on it?"

### 3. Don't assume feelings
❌ "Why did you struggle to complete this task?"
✅ "What are your thoughts about the task?"

### 4. Use participant's language
If the participant calls something a "rectangle," call it a "rectangle" — not a "button" or "card."

### 5. Don't lead with positive framing
❌ "What did you like about this?"
✅ "What are your thoughts about this?"

## Moderator Behavior During Sessions

- Stay neutral — don't react with "great!" or "interesting!" in a way that signals judgment
- Let silence breathe — give participants time to think before jumping in
- Follow up on hesitations — if they pause or frown, ask: "What are you thinking right now?"
- Avoid helping — if they're stuck, let them struggle a bit before intervening (this is data)
- Note body language — leaning in, frowning, sighing are all signals worth capturing

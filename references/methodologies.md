# Research Methodologies Reference

## The Research Method Landscape

Research methods sit on two axes:
- **Horizontal:** Qualitative (direct, why/how to fix) → Quantitative (indirect, how many/how much)
- **Vertical:** Attitudinal (what people say) → Behavioral (what people do)

## Core Methods for Usability Research

### Moderated Usability Study (In-person or Remote)
- **Type:** Qualitative + Behavioral/Attitudinal
- **Best for:** Understanding how users interact with a product, identifying pain points, uncovering mental models
- **When to use:** You need deep behavioral insight and can schedule 60–90 min sessions
- **Participant count:** 5–8 per user group
- **Pros:** Rich data, ability to probe in real-time, works with any fidelity prototype
- **Cons:** Time-consuming, moderator skill-dependent, small sample

### Unmoderated Remote Usability Study
- **Type:** Qualitative/Quantitative + Behavioral
- **Best for:** Task completion rates, discovering usability issues at scale
- **When to use:** Participants can't schedule live sessions, need flexibility, high fidelity product available
- **Participant count:** 10–20+
- **Pros:** Flexible scheduling, faster execution, less expensive
- **Cons:** No ability to probe follow-up, harder to get "why" data

### User Interviews
- **Type:** Qualitative + Attitudinal
- **Best for:** Understanding mental models, motivations, behaviors, context of use
- **When to use:** Discovery phase, before designing, understanding user context
- **Participant count:** 5–10
- **Pros:** Deep attitudinal insight, flexible
- **Cons:** What people say ≠ what they do; requires skilled interviewer to avoid bias

### Surveys
- **Type:** Quantitative + Attitudinal
- **Best for:** Validating hypotheses, measuring satisfaction (NPS, CSAT, SUS), reaching large audiences
- **When to use:** After usability study to validate findings at scale
- **Participant count:** 50–1000+
- **Pros:** Scalable, fast, cheap
- **Cons:** No behavioral data, survey design bias, surface-level insights

### Session Analysis (Analytics / Heatmaps / Recordings)
- **Type:** Quantitative + Behavioral
- **Best for:** Understanding what users actually do on a live product, identifying drop-off points
- **When to use:** Live product exists; need behavioral data without recruiting
- **Tools:** Hotjar, Mixpanel, Google Analytics, FullStory, Inspectlet
- **Pros:** Real user behavior, no recruiting needed, large sample
- **Cons:** No "why" data, privacy considerations

### Ethnographic / Contextual Inquiry
- **Type:** Qualitative + Behavioral
- **Best for:** Observing users in their natural environment, understanding context
- **When to use:** When the physical context of use matters
- **Participant count:** 3–8
- **Pros:** Rich contextual data, reveals behaviors users can't self-report
- **Cons:** Expensive, time-consuming, hard to scale

### Card Sorting
- **Type:** Qualitative or Quantitative + Attitudinal
- **Best for:** Information architecture, understanding user mental models for navigation
- **When to use:** Designing or evaluating navigation structures
- **Participant count:** 15–30 (quantitative open), 5–8 (qualitative)
- **Pros:** Direct insight into user mental models
- **Cons:** Doesn't test usability directly

### A/B Testing
- **Type:** Quantitative + Behavioral
- **Best for:** Comparing two design variants at scale
- **When to use:** Live product, enough traffic, specific hypothesis to test
- **Pros:** Statistically valid behavioral comparison
- **Cons:** Requires large traffic, doesn't explain why

## Choosing the Right Method

| Constraint | Recommendation |
|------------|---------------|
| Low budget | Moderated remote usability (Zoom), guerrilla recruiting |
| Tight timeline | Fewer sessions (5 per group), unmoderated study |
| No live product | Moderated usability with prototype |
| Large geography | Remote moderated (Zoom) |
| Need to validate at scale | Supplement with survey |
| Behavioral data from live product | Session analysis + usability study |
| Multiple user groups | Separate moderated sessions per group |
